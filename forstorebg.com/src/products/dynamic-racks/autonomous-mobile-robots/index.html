System description

AMRs (autonomous mobile robots) are intelligent transport devices that move independently within a warehouse or production space, without the need for pre-built routes. They use virtual maps, sensors and software to determine the most efficient path between different points.

Unlike traditional automated systems, these robots can make decisions in real time, adapting to their environment and avoiding obstacles. This makes them ideal for dynamic warehouses where conditions and flows frequently change.

AMR systems are widely used in modern intralogistics, automating the transportation of pallets, cartons, and various cargo, reducing the need for manual labor and increasing the efficiency of operations.

Key benefits

One of the biggest advantages of AMR is their flexibility. They do not require physical infrastructure such as rails or magnetic lines, allowing for rapid implementation and easy adaptation to changes in the warehouse.

The system is scalable – as workloads increase, new robots can be added without the need for major infrastructure changes. This makes AMR extremely suitable for businesses with growing or seasonal workloads.

Additionally, AMRs improve accuracy and reduce human error by automating repetitive tasks and optimizing order processing processes.

How autonomous mobile robots work

AMR begins by creating a digital map of the warehouse by scanning the space. This map is used by all robots in the system to navigate and perform tasks.

Each robot receives tasks from a central management software (fleet management system), which optimizes the movement of all devices and distributes tasks based on load and distances. This allows for maximum efficiency and avoidance of traffic jams.

Using technologies such as LiDAR sensors, cameras and ultrasonic sensors, AMR robots can detect people and objects and change their route in real time, ensuring safe operation in a shared environment.

Types of AMR robots

There are different types of AMRs depending on their intended use. Some are designed to transport light loads such as boxes, cartons and containers, while others can carry pallets weighing up to 1500 kg.

There are models with built-in conveyors for automatic load transfer, as well as those with lifting platforms for handling pallets and racking systems. This allows integration with various warehouse solutions.

Some AMRs are designed to work together with operators (collaborative robots), and can follow them or hand them goods during the picking process.

Comparison with AGV systems

The main difference between AMR and AGV (Automated Guided Vehicles) is the way they navigate. AGVs follow pre-defined routes, while AMRs move freely and choose the optimal path in real time.

This makes AMRs significantly more flexible and suitable for dynamic environments where warehouse layouts can change. AGV systems are better suited for stable and repeatable processes.

Overall, AMRs are considered the next evolution in warehouse automation, delivering higher efficiency, adaptability, and lower long-term operating costs.

Areas of application

AMRs are actively used for the internal transport of goods – from receiving and warehousing to shipping. They can replace or complement forklifts, conveyors and other traditional systems.

In picking processes, robots can deliver goods directly to operators, which significantly reduces processing time and increases productivity.

In a manufacturing environment, AMRs are used to supply lines with materials and components, ensuring continuous flow and better synchronization between different stages of production.

Choosing an AMR solution

Investing in AMR systems leads to significant improvements in productivity and optimization of logistics processes. They reduce transportation times, increase order processing speed and minimize errors.

Thanks to easy integration, AMR can be implemented in both new and existing warehouses without major infrastructure changes, making it a cost-effective solution for many companies.

With the development of technology and increasing demands on logistics, AMRs are becoming a key element of modern automated warehouses and industrial systems.
